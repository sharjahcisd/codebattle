Where can they be?

On our bodies In our clothes Inside our bodies
Common smart devices that are worn on one's body include wireless earphones and headphones and smart watches. These devices are small, and if they had to do a lot of heavy computing and other intensive tasks like cellular radio communication, they would quickly run out of charge. That's why they usually pair with a cellphone, delegating these tasks to it. This gives the convenience of having a device on one's body, and the power of a cellphone. Sometimes, these devices are used without the wearer's consent. For example, people who're sentenced to home arrest, and those who're made to quarantine as a result of a pandemic, are sometimes made to wear tracker wristbands. It is important to take into consideration the ethical considerations of having an intrusive device nonconsensually installed on someone's body. A convenient aspect of having a device on one's body is that they can measure some of a person's physical characteristics. Smart watches and especially fitness bands use this to their advantage, tracking a person's heart rate, blood pressure, athletic and sleep activity and other relevant characteristics. Smart watches have even been known to save people's lives, automatically calling emergency services if they detect dangerous conditions.
Some smart devices are installed on our clothes, and some manufacturers are even making clothes that are themselves the smart device. These devices, like those installed on the body, also have access to a person's physical characteristics, but this time they are better suited for tracking physical activity like running. They can also protect the clothes themselves, like anti-theft RF based devices.
Another class of smart devices are installed inside our bodies! These are usually not powered by batteries, but rather obtain their power from nearby radiation by devices that are trying to interact with these devices. They usually work using RFID communication, and most of the time are used to store data about a person. This extends to pets as well, with many domestic pets being microchipped for identification. Some companies are even trying to get a digital Covid-pass installed inside people's bodies, so that they can access venues that restrict access to vaccinated individuals only. Like with other wearable devices, there are sensitive ethical concerns associated with these devices, and especially when someone is coerced to install them inside their bodies without their full and informed consent.
